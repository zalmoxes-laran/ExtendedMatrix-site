## Abstract

This study presents a digital workflow for the diachronic
reconstruction and virtual re-contextualization of fresco
palimpsests in the Santa Maria in Via Lata hypogeum, Rome. By
integrating TLS and photogrammetric data, historical documentation,
stratigraphic interpretation and the **Extended Matrix protocol**,
the workflow reconstructs the 6th-century architectural setting as
a spatial framework for the decorative phases between the 6th and
11th centuries. It formalises the links between surviving masonry,
detached fresco fragments and virtual stratigraphic units, making
explicit the evidential chain from measured data to reconstruction
hypotheses. The result is a transparent methodology for fragmented,
displaced and stratigraphically superimposed heritage evidence.

## Why it matters for EM

The paper is a case study in applying EM to a **palimpsest**
condition — where the archaeological record is not a single
sequence but multiple superimposed decorative phases on the same
surfaces, further complicated by detached fragments that have
migrated from their original locations. The Extended Matrix formal
apparatus (VSUs, virtual stratigraphic relationships, source-based
provenance) provides the notation to keep the diachronic
hypotheses distinguishable from the surviving evidence and from
each other, across six centuries of superimposition.

The workflow also demonstrates the integration between
reality-based capture (terrestrial laser scanning + photogrammetry)
and reconstructive modelling anchored by the EM graph, an
end-to-end that the Extended Matrix ecosystem is explicitly
designed to support.
